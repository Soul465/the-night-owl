How Frank Ocean's "Self Control" Uses Jazz Harmony in R&B

By Cameron / Gramercy · May 2026

"Self Control" is in A-flat major. Most producers wouldn't guess that. The song sounds sad. It feels like it should be in a minor key. But Frank wrote it in a bright major key and got it to land as one of the most emotionally devastating tracks of the 2010s.

The mechanism behind that move is jazz harmony. Specifically, a four-chord progression that does something tricky with what it implies and what it actually delivers. Once you see what's happening, you can use the same technique in your own beats.

The Progression

Self Control's verse uses four chords:

A♭maj7 → Fm7 → B♭m7 → E♭7

In Roman numerals, that's Imaj7 → vi7 → ii7 → V7. This is one of the most important chord progressions in modern R&B and jazz-influenced hip-hop. Mac Miller uses it. Tyler uses it. D'Angelo uses it. Robert Glasper builds entire arrangements out of it.

If you want to play it in an easier key, capo to fret 1 and play it as Gmaj7 → Em7 → Am7 → D7 — same chord shapes, same sound, easier hands. That's how most guitar tabs of the song are written.

Why It Sounds Sad in a Major Key

The first move is the killer: Imaj7 → vi7. In our G-capo version, that's Gmaj7 dropping to Em7.

Em7 is the relative minor of G major. Same notes — just centered differently. When you go Imaj7 → vi7, you're going from "the bright home chord" to "the same key signature but read as minor." The chord doesn't change much physically. Just the bass moves down a third. But the emotional register flips from open to melancholy in one move.

This is the sentimental drop. It's the same trick behind a thousand pop songs. The reason "I Will Always Love You" hits, the reason Coldplay songs hit, the reason "Tears in Heaven" hits. Major chord drops to relative minor without warning. Your ear hears it as sadness emerging from inside brightness.

The V7 Climax

The last chord in the progression is the dramatic one: D7 (or E♭7 in the original key). That's the V7 — the dominant seventh chord built on the fifth scale degree.

V7 is the most tense chord in any major key. Three things give it that climactic pull:

  • It contains the leading tone — the 7th scale degree, one half-step below tonic. Your ear hears it as wanting to rise back home.
  • It contains the dominant scale degree — the 5th, which has the strongest harmonic pull in Western music.
  • The added 7th creates another unresolved interval.

All three notes "point home" simultaneously. That's why V7 always wants to resolve to I — back to Gmaj7 in our case. And when the loop cycles back, that's exactly what happens. The D7 at the end of each verse pulls you back to Gmaj7 at the start of the next. This is the engine of the song's gravity. The pull-home tension is what makes you want to keep listening.

The Secondary Dominant Move (Where the Yearning Lives)

Here's where Self Control gets sophisticated. Look at that V7 chord again — D7 in our capo-G version. In G major, D7 (D-F#-A-C) is the dominant chord. It wants to resolve to G major. That's the obvious move.

But Frank does something subtler. The D7 doesn't always feel like it wants to go to G. Listen to the chorus closely — in certain sections, the D7 feels like it wants to resolve somewhere else. That's because D7 is also functionally the dominant chord of G major's relative minor (E minor). The same chord serves two masters: pulling to home (G) and pulling to the shadow (Em).

This is called tonal ambiguity. The progression refuses to commit. Every time D7 hits, your ear isn't sure whether it's going to resolve up to brightness or down to sadness. That uncertainty is the yearning. It's the same mechanism behind the song's lyrical theme — wanting someone but not quite being able to have them, watching someone leave but staying in the room.

If you want to feel this in your own playing: loop Gmaj7 → Em7 → Am7 → D7. When the D7 hits, deliberately try to go to Em7 next instead of Gmaj7. Listen to how the same chord can imply two different resolutions. That's the harmonic mechanism Frank built the song around.

The Deceptive Cadence Variant

In multiple sections of Self Control, the loop closes on a chord you wouldn't expect. Instead of D7 → Gmaj7 (the standard V7 → I resolution), Frank sometimes lands on Em7 instead. This is called a deceptive cadence.

Your ear is set up to expect home (Gmaj7) at the end of each cycle. When you get the relative minor (Em7) instead, you get emotional whiplash without pain. The progression promised brightness. It delivered shadow. That gap between expectation and delivery IS the song's sentimentality.

This is one of the most important harmonic techniques in modern emotional music. The Beatles use it constantly ("Yesterday" ends on a deceptive cadence). Eric Clapton's "Tears in Heaven" uses it. Japanese pop has a formalized progression called the "Royal Road" (IV-V-iii-vi) that's basically the deceptive cadence as a standardized loop — that's why so many anime themes and city-pop tracks have a similar emotional texture.

How to Apply This in Your Own Beats

Here are three concrete moves you can use immediately:

Move 1: Use Imaj7 → vi7 as a "warmth opener"

Start any major-key beat with Imaj7 → vi7. In C major, that's Cmaj7 → Am7. Loop it for 4 bars. Now you've set up the sentimental register before any drums, bass, or melody enter. The harmonic foundation is doing the emotional work for you. You can be aggressive with the lyrics on top because the chords are carrying the weight.

Move 2: End your verse loop on vi instead of I

If you have a 4-chord progression that resolves to I, try ending it on vi (the relative minor) instead. C → F → G → C (typical pop loop) becomes C → F → G → Am (deceptive cadence). The exact same 4 chords feel completely different. The deceptive version creates a "the song isn't done with you" feeling that makes listeners stay engaged.

Move 3: Layer maj7 and m7 extensions on every chord

Most hip-hop chord progressions use plain triads. Frank uses 7th extensions on every chord. Plain C becomes Cmaj7. Plain Am becomes Am7. Plain F becomes Fmaj7. Plain G becomes G7. Same progression, sophisticated sound. The maj7 adds warmth. The m7 adds soul. The dominant 7 adds tension. Stacking 7ths on the four basic chord types is the single fastest way to upgrade your harmonic vocabulary from "trap" to "Mac Miller / Tyler / Frank."

Related Progressions Worth Knowing

The same Imaj7 – vi7 – ii7 – V7 architecture shows up across the modern R&B and jazz-rap canon. A few you should know:

  • SZA — "Doves in the Wind" (feat. Kendrick Lamar) — In B-flat minor, but uses a similar four-chord cycle with a secondary dominant move on the last chord. Same tonal-ambiguity mechanism Frank uses.
  • Tyler the Creator — "See You Again" — Maj7 / m7 harmony with deceptive resolution. Tyler's Flower Boy era is built on this vocabulary.
  • Mac Miller — "Self Care" — Descending diatonic with extensions. Same family of moves.
  • D'Angelo — "Untitled (How Does It Feel)" — The textbook jazz-R&B progression in pure form. Worth studying as the upstream source.

The Real Lesson

The four chords in Self Control aren't unique. Thousands of songs use Imaj7 → vi7 → ii7 → V7. What makes the song special is how Frank uses the progression — the tonal ambiguity in the V7, the occasional deceptive resolution, the way the melody sits over those specific chord tones, the warm guitar and rhodes voicings, his vocal phrasing across the chord changes.

If you want to write something that hits like Self Control hits, you don't need to invent new chord progressions. You need to take the same Imaj7 – vi7 – ii7 – V7 progression and find your way of using it. What instruments. What tempo. What you put on top. What your version of the deceptive cadence sounds like. What your tonal ambiguity feels like.

Producers like Frank, Mac, Tyler, and D'Angelo aren't operating with secret chord knowledge. They're operating with the same chord vocabulary as everyone else — they just use it deliberately. That's the work.

Try this exercise tonight: Open FL Studio. Load a Rhodes preset (Scarbee Mark I from Komplete or any electric piano you have). Set the tempo to 88 BPM. Loop Cmaj7 → Am7 → Dm7 → G7 for 4 bars. Just listen. Don't add drums yet. Don't add bass. Let the chords sit. After 5 minutes, you'll start hearing why this progression has been the foundation of soul, R&B, and jazz-influenced hip-hop for fifty years.
